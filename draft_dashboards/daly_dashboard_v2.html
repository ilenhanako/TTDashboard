Disease Category Reference Guide

A · Infectious & Parasitic Diseases
Diseases caused by pathogenic microorganisms — bacteria, viruses, parasites, and fungi — that can be transmitted directly or indirectly between individuals or from the environment.
Key examples: Tuberculosis (TB), diarrhoeal diseases, lower respiratory infections (pneumonia), HIV/AIDS, malaria, dengue fever, hepatitis B & C, meningitis, encephalitis, childhood-cluster diseases (measles, whooping cough), intestinal worms
B · Maternal, Perinatal & Nutritional
Conditions arising during pregnancy, childbirth, and the neonatal period, as well as nutritional deficiencies that impair healthy development and functioning across the life course.
Key examples: Maternal haemorrhage and sepsis, preterm birth complications, birth asphyxia, neonatal sepsis, protein-energy malnutrition, iron-deficiency anaemia, vitamin A & iodine deficiency, sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)
C · Noncommunicable Diseases (NCDs)
Chronic, non-infectious conditions that typically progress slowly and persist over long periods. They are the leading cause of death and disability globally, driven heavily by lifestyle and ageing.
Key examples: Cardiovascular diseases (heart attack, coronary artery disease), stroke, cancers / malignant neoplasms (lung, liver, colorectal, breast, stomach), diabetes mellitus, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, other chronic respiratory diseases
D · Injuries (incl. War)
Health loss resulting from external causes — both unintentional accidents and deliberate acts of violence. Injuries disproportionately affect working-age adults and young people.
Key examples: Road traffic accidents, falls, drowning, burns, poisoning (unintentional); interpersonal violence, homicide, collective violence and war; occupational injuries
E · Mental & Substance Disorders
Conditions that affect mood, thinking, and behaviour, causing significant impairment in functioning. These conditions are often under-reported and under-treated, particularly in lower-income settings.
Key examples: Depressive disorders, anxiety disorders, self-harm and suicide, eating disorders (anorexia, bulimia), childhood behavioural disorders (ADHD, conduct disorder), schizophrenia, bipolar disorder
F · Substance Abuse
Health burden attributable specifically to the harmful use of psychoactive substances, including dependence, withdrawal, and substance-induced organ damage.
Key examples: Alcohol use disorders (cirrhosis, alcohol dependence), drug use disorders (opioid dependence, stimulant use, cannabis use disorder)
G · Others
A residual category of diseases not captured in categories A–F, covering a wide range of chronic non-communicable and organ-specific conditions that nevertheless contribute substantially to the total burden.
Key examples: Neurological conditions (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, epilepsy), musculoskeletal diseases (osteoarthritis, low back pain, rheumatoid arthritis), digestive diseases (cirrhosis, peptic ulcer), sense organ diseases (vision and hearing loss), genitourinary diseases (chronic kidney disease), skin diseases, endocrine & blood disorders, STDs excl. HIV, leprosy
Unclassified Residual
A small residual that appears for some countries (up to ~6% for India) where the sum of WHO category parent rows does not account for the All-Causes total. This is an artefact of the WHO GHE spreadsheet structure where some sub-disease rows are tabulated outside their parent category totals.
Affected countries & share: India (5.6%), Myanmar (4.9%), China (3.4%), Viet Nam (3.1%), Lao PDR (3.7%)
